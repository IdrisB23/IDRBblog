Mathematical Induction,
Dominoes, Sheep, And All That Jazz

once, then twice, again, and over again...

by Idris Babay on June 3rd, 2020, 7min reads

Mathematical induction is at the heart of most proofs. It's a simple but a very powerful technique. We use it to generalize an observation made locally.

Let's start with an example. Let's take our beloved domino pieces out and put them in a perfectly aligned row. Notice that now we can apply some kind of order to the pieces. We simply label the first piece in line with d_1, second with d_2, and in general i-th with d_i.

We push the first piece on our row onto the second. We notice that these two are falling down already. We note F(d_i) to convey that d_i has fallen. We know that if a piece falls onto another, it will bring it down with it. If you're not convinced, try it for yourself. So the satement: F(d_i) implies F(d_i+1) holds.

We conclude that all pieces must fall after some time. Formally, for all natural numbers n < m+1, d_1, d_2, ..., d_n-1, and d_n are down, whereas m is the total number of domino pieces we had from the start.

Some may ask, why is this even a thing? You may be able to count all the domino pieces that have fallen and check, whether it adds up the whole count. But imagine we were dealing with an infinite number of pieces. Of course this wouldn't be possible in our case, but in mathematics there are no such limitations and infinite models are fairly common.

In General

We generalize the proof technique. A proof by induction over a set of elements { m_1, m_2, ..., m_n} consists of:

i) Basis: the statement is true for some elements m_s, s > 0 (local observation).

ii) Step: assume statement holds for some index i > s, and prove it still holds for element i+1.

Can you spot the pattern? d_1 implies d_2 implies ... d_n. This saves us a lot of writing effort and helps us establish a useful property over a sequence of elements regardless, whether it's short, long, or even infinite.

Non-Example

Non-examples can sometimes help us grasp concepts even better than examples do.

Somebody just went out and said: "All sheep in a group have the same color". Since I've just learned about mathematical indution, I wanted show off my new skills and attempted to prove his claim. I wrote:

Basis: If we take a group having one sheep. The statement is trivially true.

Step: Suppose it's true for group of n-1 sheep, we want to prove that it holds for a group of n sheep. Let's take a group of n sheep and call it M. We'll take the first sheep s_1 out of M, now we have M'={s_2, ... s_n} having n-1 sheep. So by our assumption all sheep in M' have the same color. We make M''={s_1, ..., s_n-1} out of M by taking out last sheep. Likewise M'' has sheep of same color. We conclude it holds for M.

Could you spot the mistake? Notice that the property doesn't hold for n=2. We have a set M consisting of one sky-blue and one pink sheep. M' would only contain the sky-blue and M'' the pink sheep. The problem is: the statement holds(trivially) for M' and M'' but we don't have a "comparison sheep" to generalize to M.
